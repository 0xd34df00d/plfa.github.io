## Introduction

In this chapter we prove that beta reduction is _confluent_, a
property also known as _Church-Rosser_. That is, if there are
reduction sequences from any term `L` to two different terms `M‚ÇÅ` and
`M‚ÇÇ`, then there exist reduction sequences from those two terms to
some common term `N`. In pictures:

        L
       / \
      /   \
     /     \
    M‚ÇÅ      M‚ÇÇ
     \     /
      \   /
       \ /
        N

where downward lines are instances of `‚Äî‚Ü†`.

Confluence is studied in many other kinds of rewrite systems besides
the lambda calculus, and it is well known how to prove confluence in
rewrite systems that enjoy the _diamond property_, a single-step
version of confluence. Let `‚áõ` be a relation.  Then `‚áõ` has the
diamond property if whenever `L ‚áõ M‚ÇÅ` and `L ‚áõ M‚ÇÇ`, then there exists
an `N` such that `M‚ÇÅ ‚áõ N` and `M‚ÇÇ ‚áõ N`. This is just an instance of
the same picture above, where downward lines are now instance of `‚áõ`.
If we write `‚áõ*` for the reflexive and transitive closure of `‚áõ`, then
confluence of `‚áõ*` follows immediately from the diamond property.

Unfortunately, reduction in the lambda calculus does not satisfy the
diamond property. Here is a counter example.

    (Œª x. x x)((Œª x. x) a) ‚Äî‚Üí (Œª x. x x) a
    (Œª x. x x)((Œª x. x) a) ‚Äî‚Üí ((Œª x. x) a) ((Œª x. x) a)

Both terms can reduce to `a a`, but the second term requires two steps
to get there, not one.

To side-step this problem, we'll define an auxilliary reduction
relation, called _parallel reduction_, that can perform many
reductions simultaneously and thereby satisfy the diamond property.
Furthermore, we show that a parallel reduction sequence exists between
any two terms if and only if a beta reduction sequence exists between them.
Thus, we can reduce the proof of confluence for beta reduction to
confluence for parallel reduction.

## Equivalence between parallel reduction and reduction

Here we prove that for any `M` and `N`, `M ‚áõ* N` if and only if `M ‚Äî‚Ü† N`.
The only-if direction is particularly easy. We start by showing

The proof is by induction on `M ‚áõ N`.

* Suppose `x ‚áõ x`. We immediately have `x ‚Äî‚Ü† x`.

* Suppose `∆õ N ‚áõ ∆õ N‚Ä≤` because `N ‚áõ N‚Ä≤`. By the induction hypothesis
  we have `N ‚Äî‚Ü† N‚Ä≤`. We conclude that `∆õ N ‚Äî‚Ü† ∆õ N‚Ä≤` because
  `‚Äî‚Ü†` is a congruence.

* Suppose `L ¬∑ M ‚áõ L‚Ä≤ ¬∑ M‚Ä≤` because `L ‚áõ L‚Ä≤` and `M ‚áõ M‚Ä≤`.
  By the induction hypothesis, we have `L ‚Äî‚Ü† L‚Ä≤` and `M ‚Äî‚Ü† M‚Ä≤`.
  So `L ¬∑ M ‚Äî‚Ü† L‚Ä≤ ¬∑ M` and then `L‚Ä≤ ¬∑ M  ‚Äî‚Ü† L‚Ä≤ ¬∑ M‚Ä≤`
  because `‚Äî‚Ü†` is a congruence. 

* Suppose `(∆õ N) ¬∑ M  ‚áõ  N‚Ä≤ [ M‚Ä≤ ]` because `N ‚áõ N‚Ä≤` and `M ‚áõ M‚Ä≤`.
  By similar reasoning, we have
  `(∆õ N) ¬∑ M ‚Äî‚Ü† (∆õ N‚Ä≤) ¬∑ M‚Ä≤`
  which we can following with the Œ≤ reduction
  `(∆õ N‚Ä≤) ¬∑ M‚Ä≤ ‚Äî‚Üí N‚Ä≤ [ M‚Ä≤ ]`.

The proof is by induction on `M ‚áõ M‚Ä≤`. The first four cases
are straightforward so we just consider the last one for `pbeta`.

* Suppose `(∆õ N) ¬∑ M  ‚áõ  N‚Ä≤ [ M‚Ä≤ ]` because `N ‚áõ N‚Ä≤` and `M ‚áõ M‚Ä≤`.
  By the induction hypothesis, we have
  `rename (ext œÅ) N ‚áõ rename (ext œÅ) N‚Ä≤` and
  `rename œÅ M ‚áõ rename œÅ M‚Ä≤`.
  So by `pbeta` we have
  `(∆õ rename (ext œÅ) N) ¬∑ (rename œÅ M) ‚áõ (rename (ext œÅ) N) [ rename œÅ M ]`.
  However, to conclude we instead need parallel reduction to
  `rename œÅ (N [ M ])`. But thankfully, renaming and substitution
  commute with one another.


With the `par-rename` lemma in hand, it is straightforward to show
that extending substitutions preserves the pointwise parallel
reduction relation.

## Parallel reduction satisfies the diamond property

The heart of the confluence proof is made of stone, or rather, of
diamond!  We show that parallel reduction satisfies the diamond
property: that if `M ‚áõ N` and `M ‚áõ N‚Ä≤`, then `N ‚áõ L` and `N‚Ä≤ ‚áõ L` for
some `L`.  The typical proof is an induction on `M ‚áõ N` and `M ‚áõ N‚Ä≤`
so that every possible pair gives rise to a witeness `L` given by
performing enough beta reductions in parallel.

However, a simpler approach is to perform as many beta reductions in
parallel as possible on `M`, say `M ‚Å∫`, and then show that `N` also
parallel reduces to `M ‚Å∫`. This is the idea of Takahashi's _complete
development_. The desired property may be illustrated as

        M
       /|
      / |
     /  |
    N   2
     \  |
      \ |
       \|
        M‚Å∫

where downward lines are instances of `‚áõ`, so we call it the _triangle
property_.

The proof of the triangle property is an induction on `M ‚áõ N`.

* Suppose `x ‚áõ x`. Clearly `x ‚Å∫ = x`, so `x ‚áõ x`. 

* Suppose `∆õ M ‚áõ ∆õ N`. By the induction hypothesis we have `N ‚áõ M ‚Å∫`
  and by definition `(Œª M) ‚Å∫ = Œª (M ‚Å∫)`, so we conclude that `Œª N ‚áõ Œª
  (M ‚Å∫)`.

* Suppose `(Œª N) ¬∑ M ‚áõ N‚Ä≤ [ M‚Ä≤ ]`. By the induction hypothesis, we have
  `N‚Ä≤ ‚áõ N ‚Å∫` and `M‚Ä≤ ‚áõ M ‚Å∫`. Since substitution respects parallel reduction,
  it follows that `N‚Ä≤ [ M‚Ä≤ ] ‚áõ N ‚Å∫ [ M ‚Å∫ ]`, but the right hand side
  is exactly `((Œª N) ¬∑ M) ‚Å∫`, hence `N‚Ä≤ [ M‚Ä≤ ] ‚áõ ((Œª N) ¬∑ M) ‚Å∫`.

* Suppose `(Œª L) ¬∑ M ‚áõ (Œª L‚Ä≤) ¬∑ M‚Ä≤`. By the induction hypothesis
  we have `L‚Ä≤ ‚áõ L ‚Å∫` and `M‚Ä≤ ‚áõ M ‚Å∫`; by definition `((Œª L) ¬∑ M) ‚Å∫ = L ‚Å∫ [ M ‚Å∫ ]`.
  It follows `(Œª L‚Ä≤) ¬∑ M‚Ä≤ ‚áõ L ‚Å∫ [ M ‚Å∫ ]`.

* Suppose `x ¬∑ M ‚áõ x ¬∑ M‚Ä≤`. By the induction hypothesis we have `M‚Ä≤ ‚áõ M ‚Å∫`
  and `x ‚áõ x ‚Å∫` so that `x ¬∑ M‚Ä≤ ‚áõ x ¬∑ M ‚Å∫`.
  The remaining case is proved in the same way, so we ignore it.  (As
  there is currently no way in Agda to expand the catch-all pattern in
  the definition of `_‚Å∫` for us before checking the right-hand side,
  we have to write down the remaining case explicitly.)

The diamond property then follows by halving the diamond into two triangles.

        M
       /|\
      / | \
     /  |  \
    N   2   N‚Ä≤
     \  |  /
      \ | /
       \|/
        M‚Å∫

That is, the diamond property is proved by applying the
triangle property on each side with the same confluent term `M ‚Å∫`.

## Proof of confluence for parallel reduction

As promised at the beginning, the proof that parallel reduction is
confluent is easy now that we know it satisfies the triangle property.
We just need to prove the strip lemma, which states that
if `M ‚áõ N` and `M ‚áõ* N‚Ä≤`, then
`N ‚áõ* L` and `N‚Ä≤ ‚áõ L` for some `L`.
The following diagram illustrates the strip lemma

        M
       / \
      1   *
     /     \
    N       N‚Ä≤
     \     /
      *   1
       \ /
        L

where downward lines are instances of `‚áõ` or `‚áõ*`, depending on how
they are marked.

The proof of the strip lemma is a straightforward induction on `M ‚áõ* N‚Ä≤`,
using the triangle property in the induction step.

## Proof of confluence for reduction

Confluence of reduction is a corollary of confluence for parallel
reduction. From
`L ‚Äî‚Ü† M‚ÇÅ` and `L ‚Äî‚Ü† M‚ÇÇ` we have
`L ‚áõ* M‚ÇÅ` and `L ‚áõ* M‚ÇÇ` by `betas-pars`.
Then by confluence we obtain some `L` such that
`M‚ÇÅ ‚áõ* N` and `M‚ÇÇ ‚áõ* N`, from which we conclude that
`M‚ÇÅ ‚Äî‚Ü† N` and `M‚ÇÇ ‚Äî‚Ü† N` by `pars-betas`.

## Notes

Broadly speaking, this proof of confluence, based on parallel
reduction, is due to W. Tait and P. Martin-Lof (see Barendredgt 1984,
Section 3.2).  Details of the mechanization come from several sources.
The `subst-par` lemma is the "strong substitutivity" lemma of Shafer,
Tebbi, and Smolka (ITP 2015). The proofs of `par-triangle`, `strip`,
and `par-confluence` are based on the notion of complete development
by Takahashi (1995) and Pfenning's 1992 technical report about the
Church-Rosser theorem. In addition, we consulted Nipkow and
Berghofer's mechanization in Isabelle, which is based on an earlier
article by Nipkow (JAR 1996).
